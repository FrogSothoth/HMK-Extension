## FEUDAL class &amp; occupation

All land is owned by a sovereign, who grants most	of	it	as	fiefs	to	vassals,	who,	in	turn,	repeat the subinfeudation process. Feudal societies survive mostly on large-scale, intensive farming.

## class &amp; occupation feudal/imperial

Social classes are identical to those of a Feudal society, except for the Nobility:

- If the initial d10 or d20 roll resulting in a Noble class is odd (97 or 9; or 19), then use the Noble subtable of the Feudal society for parent occupation.
- If the initial d10 or d20 roll is even (98 or 0; or 20), then use the Noble subtable of the Imperial society for parent occupation (page 28).

## IMPERIAL class &amp; occupation

A centralist and bureaucratic society developed from and retaining features of feudal, tributary, and even tribal peoples. A noble class exists in most Imperial societies, but the rulers also make great	use	of	appointed	officers	(governors).

## city-state class &amp; occupation

An	oligarchy	defined	more	by	wealth than land ownership, emphasising trade and-in many realms throughout the Venarian Sea-slavery. The geographical boundary of a city-state is usually smaller than that of an empire, while its urban population is usually greater.

## city-state urban

- When	making	the	d100	Background Roll	for	any	Occupation	in	a	City-State society,	roll	two	tens	dice	and	use	the lower result along with the one units die	to	determine	the	result.	This	yields	a higher	chance	of	an	Urban	Background.

Communal and clan-based society led by a chieftain.	Subsistence	methods	include	foraging, herding,	fishing,	and	small-scale	farming-each present in varying degrees according to the geography of the people's homeland.

## Background

After establishing Parent Occupation for characters, determine their family's Background-whether they resided in a sizeable settlement or in the countryside. There are four possible Backgrounds: Urban, City, Rural, or Wilderness. Each provides special opportunities for skill development in step 12.

Make	d100	Background	Roll.

Make a d100 roll against the Urban chance corresponding to the Parent Occupation determined in step 7, unless the character is from a nonSettled Tribal Society. Even if the Urban chance is a o or 100, make the d100 roll anyway-see the City and Wilderness rules, below, and the examples in the sidebar.

## city

If the d100 Background roll ends in a 0 or 5 and also produces an Urban result, then the character's home settlement is particularly cosmopolitan, usually with a large Market Size (5+). This is called an Urban (City) background.

## Rural Background

The vast majority of folk in Venarive live in the countryside or in small villages usually of no more than a couple of hundred people. If the d100 roll is higher than the Parent Occupation's Urban chance, then the character has a Rural Background.

## wilderness

If the character's Parent Occupation has an Urban chance of 10 or less , then a d100 Background roll ending in a 0 or 5 that also produces a Rural result means the character comes from a very isolated, perhaps rugged, location. This is called a Rural (Wilderness) background.

Tribal Background : characters from a Tribal Society automatically have the Rural (Wilderness) background. For Settled Tribal folk, background is rolled for randomly, just like with any other non-Tribal character.

## kinship roll

Characters may seek favour or help from their family during the campaign. Such a connection could in part be resolved with a Kinship Roll, a d100 test against the character's Estrangement score. The GM could make the test in secretand use the result as a guide to shape the actual roleplaying of the situation-or have the player make it at a crucial moment of the request.

## kinship roll

- cf Character denied the favour or help and standing	erodes	(-10	penalty	to	future Kinship	Rolls	until	one	is	successful). Unpopular	and	certainly	outcast	kin	suffer aggressive	rebuke	or	revenge	because	of some	past	offense.
