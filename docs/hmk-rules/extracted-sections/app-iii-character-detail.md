# Appendix III: Character Detail

## appendix iii: character detail

Expanding on the information in the Character chapter,	players	may give	further	detail	to	their	PC's appearance , handedness ,	and background story .	Roll	on	the	following	tables	to	determine	these	characteristics or	simply	select	results	within	the	given	parameters.

## appearance

Character	appearance	not	only	includes	the	Comeliness	attribute	but also	complexion,	hair	colour,	eye	colour,	and	build	(physical	stature).

## Build

People	with	a	larger	stature	are	generally	stronger.	The	Ælrhin	and Kuzhai	defy	this	rule	compared	to	humans	but	express	it	similarly relative	to	themselves.	To	determine	a	character's	precise	height	and weight,	refer	to	the Character Build table (page 428) and follow these four steps:

- [1]  Find	the	character's	Strength	(STR)	score	in	the	left-side	subtable. This is STR before the	Folk	modifier	is	applied	(page	38).
- [2]  Roll	a	d100	in	the	section	corresponding	to	the	character's	STR and	compare	it	to	the	appropriate	Folk	column.
- Ænarin and Malakta	use	the	Human	column.
- Nadi	use	the	Ælrhin	column	but	add	an	additional	10	pounds.
- [3]  The	character's	height	in	inches	is	the	first	resulting	number.
- [4]  The	character's	weight	in	pounds	is	the	second	resulting	number; then	add	an	additional	3d6	pounds.

Build Traits :	refer	to	the	right-side	subtable.	The	character's	height	in inches	in	the	first	column	gives	the	corresponding Height	Trait in the second	column.	Locate	the	weight	in	the	ranges	of	the	height's	row; the corresponding column header gives the Weight Trait .

Note	the	character's	build	traits-descriptors	like	these	are	often used	in-world	by	the	characters	themselves.	Someone's	height	could be described as small , short , average , tall ,	or great ; their frame as scant , light , medium , heavy ,	or massive .

Strength	(STR)	is	largely defined by	a	character's	weight.	The	STR score	is	generated	first	in	Chapter	Two,	and	then	the	above	table is referenced to determine a precise weight within a range that realistically reflects the	given	score.	Thus,	humans	who	are	70"	tall	and weigh	165	lb	generally	have	STR	between	11	and	14;	their	stature	is usually	too	large	to	be	weaker	and	too	small	to	be	stronger.

For	brevity,	each	STR	score	in	the	table	has	only	six	rows	of variable	heights.	It	is	conceivable	that	sections	could	be	expanded	by additional	rows	to	capture	more	outliers.	As	it	is,	however,	to	model a	greater	variety,	first	determine	weight	with	the	usual	3d6	roll	(or	by selecting	a	precise	result	using	the	same	range).	Then,	to	get	an	even lighter frame ,	reduce	enough	weight	for	the	given	height	to	generate the	desired	frame.	Every	8	lb	reduced	in	this	way	also	lowers	STR	by	1.

Example :	a	player	rolls	a	character	with	STR 10,	envisioned	as	having	a	tall, gaunt	stature.	The	tallest	result	for	STR 10	in	the	table	is	71"	with	a	base	147 lb weight. Even selecting a 3 (without actually rolling 3d6) leaves the Tall character with a Light frame (150 lb in the above right table). The player reduces this by 16 lb to 134 lb (Scant/'gaunt'), and the STR to 8.

More	weight	can	be	added,	too,	but	this	does	not	increase	STR.	A character	with	STR 14	could	be	70"	tall	and	weigh	180	lb	( heavy frame). Increasing	the	weight	by	10	or	20	lb	gives	a massive frame but does not	change	STR-the	character	is	simply	corpulent.
